European Mantis (Mantis religiosa)

This beauty, a female green European mantis is a highly evolved killing machine. You can see her eyes trained on the camera waiting to either flee or strike, and her powerful raptorial arms poised at the ready.

Angle Wing Katydid (Microcentrum rhombifolium)

At first, I thought that this katdid was a leaf. This beautiful species uses cryptomimicry to act and look like a harmless leaf. It is a peaceful vegetarian, happily chomping on deciduous tree leaves. Once this female calmed down, I got some very good shots while she methodically groomed her legs and antennae with her mouthparts.

European mantis (Mantis religiosa)

This European mantis nymph is a brown variant found commonly. As you can see, its wings aren't developed fully. I took this shot while she munched on a Snowy Tree Cricket, as you can see. The attitude and posture of this killer can be almost inspirational.

Golden Huntsman Spider (Olios fasciculatus)

Huntsman spiders, like this female, are notorious for their unpredictability, even among hobbyists like me. She is a nightmarishly good hunter, hence the name, and will take on creatures twice her diminutive size. Huntsman spiders use sensory hairs on their legs to detect extremely small changes in wind and air pressure, signaling the arrival of prey. Once the food is detected, she will lunge at it without notice, and skillfully kill it with a venemous bite to the head. A few days after this photo was taken, she laid an egg sac. See if you can find her in this shot!

Elegant Crab Spider (Xysticus elegans)

There is something very unnerving about this spider. The way she stares and sits still, waiting for food to come near. Seeing this spider hunt is like the ultimate suspense thriller. Once prey is in range, she will slowly open her beartrap-like arms. Somehow she can position herslf so that the killing blow is delivered to the prey's head. Slowly, the food wanders closer, until it is right in front of her. Then, in the blink of an eye, those scary, spiked arms snap shut, and it's all over.

Western Lynx Spider (Oxyopes scalaris)

Frankly, this spider scares me. The lynx spider is a very fickle spider, and can turn on a dime. It can run and jump when frightened, which is basically whenever I come near her. In this shot, she jumped to the bottom of her enclosure and stood there, legs curled up in hunting position. You can see the remains of her previous meal, a small grasshopper, in front of her. But when these maniacs calm down, you can really see their deadlier side. They excel in hunting flies and other flying insects. They have long, black sensory hairs on their legs which detect the slightest wingbeat. As the fly flies nearer, she lunges out and takes it out of the air.
